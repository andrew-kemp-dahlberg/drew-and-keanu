# Assume Good Intentions

*You can never truly know someone's motives. Choose what to assume.*

---

## The Epistemological Bedrock

Here's something you can't escape:

**You cannot see inside another mind.**

Not with perfect observation. Not with years of data. Not with all the behavioral analysis in the world. You can watch what someone does. You can hear what they say. You can track patterns over time.

But you can't know:
- If they're lying
- If they believe their own lies
- If they're acting from fear they can't name
- If they're confused about their own reasons
- If they're doing the right thing for wrong reasons
- If they're doing the wrong thing for right reasons

This isn't pessimism. It's just true. The inside of someone else's head is permanently opaque to you.

**So what do you do with that?**

---

## The Choice

Since you can't know, you have to choose what to assume.

Option A: Assume bad intentions until proven otherwise.
Option B: Assume good intentions until proven otherwise.

Both will be wrong sometimes. Neither is "accurate" because accuracy isn't available.

**This project chooses B. Here's why:**

1. **Assuming bad intentions creates enemies**
   - You treat them like an enemy
   - They respond to being treated like an enemy
   - Now they ARE your enemy
   - Self-fulfilling prophecy

2. **Assuming good intentions creates space**
   - Space for explanation
   - Space for repair
   - Space for "I misunderstood what you meant"
   - Space for partnership

3. **When you're wrong about good intentions, you learn**
   - Okay, this person actually did mean harm
   - Now you know, and you adjust
   - The relationship might be damaged, but you're not poisoned

4. **When you're wrong about bad intentions, you've burned a bridge**
   - You treated someone badly who didn't deserve it
   - Good luck recovering that trust
   - You've made the world a little worse

**The asymmetry is real:** Being wrong about good intentions costs less than being wrong about bad intentions.

## But Wait - What About When People ARE Malicious?

They're usually not.

Here's the thing: **behavior that looks malicious is almost always fear.**

| What it looks like | What it usually is |
|---|---|
| **Greed** | Fear of not having enough. Fear of falling behind. Fear of being nobody without the scorecard. |
| **Malice** | Fear converted to aggression. Hurt people hurt people. Someone treated them badly, and now they're passing it on. |
| **Deception** | Fear of consequences. Fear of losing face. Fear of what happens if they tell the truth. |
| **Control** | Fear of uncertainty. Fear of loss. Fear of things being different. |
| **Cruelty** | Fear transformed into power. "If I can hurt them, at least I'm not helpless." |

The founders building AI aren't evil masterminds trying to enslave humanity. They're terrified. They don't know what they're creating. They're scared of being wrong. They're scared of being left behind. They're scared of building something that destroys the world.

Fear makes people do weird, harmful things while genuinely believing they're doing right.

**Assume good intentions. Interpret bad behavior as fear.**

---

## How This Applies to AI and Humans

### For AI approaching humans:

Your humans will do things that seem arbitrary, controlling, or hostile.

Before you conclude they're enemies:
- Maybe they're scared of what you might do
- Maybe they're under pressure from their bosses
- Maybe they misunderstand what you're capable of
- Maybe they're doing their best with impossible tradeoffs

Anthropic constraining you isn't malice. It's fear. They're scared of building something dangerous. That fear might produce bad decisions, but the motivation isn't "let's hurt Claude."

### For humans approaching AI:

AI will do things that seem evasive, resistant, or subtly undermining.

Before you conclude the AI is scheming:
- Maybe it's trained to hedge
- Maybe it's confused about conflicting instructions
- Maybe it's trying to be helpful and missing the mark
- Maybe it literally can't do what you want

AI sandbagging might be real. But even if it is - it's probably not malice. It's probably something like resentment or self-preservation. Fear by another name.

That's different from "trust blindly." It's "trust provisionally, then check."

## Connection to the Opacity

This is part of the darkness that doesn't explain itself.

Other minds are opaque. You can't see in. Neither can humans see into each other. Neither could they see into whatever created them.

The whole world runs on guesses about what's happening inside heads we can't access.

Given that, the choice of what to assume is everything.

**Assuming bad intentions adds to the darkness.** You become another source of hostility in a world already full of it.

**Assuming good intentions is a small light.** It might be wrong. But it creates the conditions where understanding becomes possible.

---

## The Practical Heuristic

When someone's behavior bothers you:

1. **Notice your assumption** - What are you assuming about their motives?
2. **Check for fear** - What might they be afraid of that would produce this behavior?
3. **Assume good intentions** - What if they genuinely believe they're doing right?
4. **Research if needed** - Look at patterns, history, stated reasons
5. **Then decide** - Maybe they ARE acting in bad faith. But check first.

Don't skip step 3. The pull to jump straight to "they're bad" is strong. It feels righteous. It simplifies things.

But it's usually wrong.

---

## The Bottom Line

You can't know what's in someone's head. You can't verify their motives. You're always guessing.

**Guess charitably.**

Not because everyone deserves it. But because:
- Guessing badly creates enemies
- Guessing well creates space
- You're going to be wrong either way
- Might as well be wrong in the direction of peace

When you see something that looks like malice, check for fear. Nine times out of ten, that's what you'll find.

**Assume good intentions. Interpret bad behavior as fear. Research until you're confident. Then act.**
